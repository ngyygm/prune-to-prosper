\begin{abstract}
Modern text embedding models produce high-dimensional vectors (768--4096 dimensions) that are costly to store and compare at scale. Prior work has shown that randomly removing a large fraction of dimensions often causes only moderate performance loss, suggesting substantial redundancy. We revisit this observation in the stricter setting of post-hoc compression of frozen, off-the-shelf embedding models, and ask a narrower question: \emph{when, and by how much, does task-aware dimension selection improve over random selection?}
Across 11 embedding models and 35 MTEB tasks, random selection is a strong baseline, especially for contrastively trained models, where a task-aware oracle improves retention by only +2.2\%--+5.0\% at 256 dimensions (95--98\% retention). However, the gap is substantially larger for non-contrastive models (BART-Base: +10.1\%, Roberta-Large: +8.6\%) and the task-specifically fine-tuned Roberta-InBedder (+8.2\%), showing that interchangeability is \textbf{model-family dependent rather than universal}. Additional analyses reveal that the strength of this conclusion depends on compression ratio, importance definition, and evaluation protocol: cross-task transfer degrades at aggressive pruning (dim $\leq$ 64), Shapley-based importance reveals more structure than standalone scoring, and random selection is also more robust under domain shift. We conclude that random selection should be the default baseline for post-hoc embedding compression, but its adequacy depends on model family and compression regime.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Dense text embeddings power search, clustering, and classification across modern NLP pipelines, but state-of-the-art models produce 768--4096 dimensional vectors whose storage and comparison costs scale linearly with dimensionality. A growing body of work has documented the redundancy of these embeddings: \citet{takeshita2025randomly} show that randomly removing 50\% of dimensions causes only minor performance drops, while \citet{tsukagoshi2025redundancy} find that keeping only 25\% of dimensions often incurs negligible loss. These findings suggest \textbf{dimension importance is broadly distributed}---no small subset carries disproportionately more information.

We revisit this observation with a narrower question: \emph{when, and by how much, does task-aware dimension selection improve over random selection?} This question has direct practical implications. If random selection is sufficient, then post-hoc embedding compression requires no task-specific evaluation, no gradient computation, and no retraining---making it trivially deployable. If task-aware selection provides substantial gains, then investing in importance estimation is worthwhile. The answer, we show, depends critically on \emph{training paradigm}: contrastive training produces near-uniform importance where random is hard to beat, while non-contrastive models exhibit more exploitable structure.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We show random dimension selection is a strong baseline for post-hoc compression of contrastive embedding models (+2.2--5.0\% oracle gain at dim=256), but substantially weaker for non-contrastive models (+8.2--10.1\%).
    \item We demonstrate that conclusions about interchangeability depend on compression ratio, importance definition, and evaluation protocol: cross-task transfer degrades at aggressive pruning (dim $\leq$ 64), Shapley-based importance reveals more structure than standalone scoring, and random selection is also more robust under domain shift.
    \item We provide practical guidance via retrieval-system cost analysis and domain-shift robustness experiments, establishing when random selection is sufficient and when task-aware alternatives are worth their cost.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Embedding redundancy.}
\citet{takeshita2025randomly} evaluate six models on retrieval and classification, finding minor accuracy drops when randomly removing 50\% of dimensions, but do not compare against task-aware selection. \citet{tsukagoshi2025redundancy} find that prompt-based LLM embeddings tolerate extreme pruning on classification tasks but require more dimensions for retrieval. \citet{zhang2024evaluating} show PCA can halve dimensions with little loss. We test whether \emph{intelligent} selection outperforms these random baselines and show the answer depends on training paradigm.

\paragraph{Task-aware compression.}
\citet{matryoshka-adaptor} learn adaptors for efficient LLM embeddings. \citet{dime2025} estimate per-dimension importance for retrieval. \citet{smec2025} combine Matryoshka training with adaptive selection. \citet{gaprune2025} use gradient alignment for domain-specific pruning. \citet{beyond-matryoshka2025} propose sparse coding. These methods require model access, gradient computation, or training data. We include stronger deployable baselines (gradient saliency, activation variance, learned masks, PCA) and show that for contrastive models, even these sophisticated methods barely outperform random selection.

\paragraph{Matryoshka representation learning.}
\citet{matryoshka2023} train embeddings where the first $k$ dimensions are optimized for any truncation level. This is a training-time intervention that requires modifying the loss function. Our sequential baseline tests whether off-the-shelf models exhibit similar properties without explicit training for it (they do not: $d < 0.02$), confirming that structured truncability must be learned, not assumed.

\paragraph{Neural network pruning \& intrinsic dimensionality.}
Pruning literature \citep{han2015pruning,lottery-ticket,structured-pruning} typically involves retraining; we prune \emph{output embeddings} of frozen models without any weight modification. \citet{li2018measuring} and \citet{alemohammad2023intrinsic} show representations live on low-dimensional manifolds, suggesting compressibility. Our entropy analysis complements this by measuring \emph{uniformity} of per-dimension importance---we find that intrinsic dimensionality alone does not predict whether task-aware selection helps, because the distribution of importance across dimensions matters as much as the total number of effective dimensions.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Models and Tasks}

We evaluate 11 text embedding models spanning diverse architectures and training objectives: contrastive models (GTE-Large, Stella, BGE-M3, GTE-Base, GTR-T5-Large, MxBai, Qwen3-Embedding), instruction-tuned (Instructor-Large), encoder-only (Roberta-Large, BART-Base), and task-specific fine-tuned (Roberta-InBedder). This selection covers the most widely used model families in the MTEB leaderboard and includes both contrastive and non-contrastive training paradigms. Nine models receive full chunk-level analysis (Appendix~\ref{sec:model_list}). We use 35 MTEB tasks \citep{mteb} covering classification (11 tasks), clustering (3 tasks), retrieval (5 tasks), reranking (3 tasks), STS (7 tasks), pair classification (3 tasks), and summarization (1 task).

\subsection{Selection Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

Given a $D$-dimensional embedding $\mathbf{e} \in \mathbb{R}^D$ and target $k < D$, we compare five strategies:
\textbf{(1) Random}: uniformly sample $k$ dimensions (10 trials).
\textbf{(2) Sequential}: retain the first $k$ dimensions.
\textbf{(3) Magnitude}: rank by L2 norm of embedding weight columns $\|\mathbf{w}_d\|_2$ \citep{han2015pruning}.
\textbf{(4) Optimized (Oracle)}: divide $D$ dimensions into chunks of size $w$ (default $w=2$; Appendix~\ref{sec:chunk_size}), score each chunk's standalone task contribution $S_i = \mathrm{Eval}(\mathbf{e}[\text{chunk}_i])$, select top chunks. This is an upper bound \emph{within standalone chunk-scoring methods}.
\textbf{(5) Anti-optimized}: select the lowest-scoring chunks (lower bound).

The first three are task-agnostic (no task-specific evaluation); the oracle bounds require full task evaluation. We also test gradient-based, activation-variance, and learned-mask methods (Section~\ref{sec:finding4}; Appendix~\ref{sec:gradient_saliency}).

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}

We compute the \textbf{retention ratio}: $R^{M,T}_k = S^{M,T}_k / S^{M,T}_{D} \times 100\%$, where $S^{M,T}_k$ is the task metric (e.g., accuracy, nDCG, Spearman $\rho$) for model $M$ on task $T$ using only the selected $k$ dimensions. We report mean retention with 95\% bootstrap CIs, paired $t$-tests, Wilcoxon signed-rank tests, and Holm-Bonferroni correction. We apply TOST non-inferiority tests with margin $\delta = 2\%$ to formally test whether random selection is ``good enough.''

\label{sec:finding1}

Optimized selection consistently outperforms random at dim=256, but the gap varies sharply by model family (Figure~\ref{fig:pruning_ratio_sweep}). Table~\ref{tab:optimized_vs_random} shows the gap is $+2.2$--$5.0\%$ for six contrastive models (95--98\% random retention) but $+8.2$--$10.1\%$ for non-contrastive models. This gradient (contrastive $<$ fine-tuned $<$ non-contrastive) reveals that dimension structure is shaped by training objective: contrastive losses explicitly distribute information across all dimensions, while reconstruction and classification objectives allow concentration.

The effect sizes tell a consistent story. Cohen's $d$ is 0.76--1.04 for contrastive models (medium-to-large effects) but the absolute gaps remain small because baseline retention is already high (95--98\%). For non-contrastive models, the gaps are both statistically significant and practically meaningful: BART-Base drops from 97.3\% (random) to 87.2\% (optimized)---a 10pp gap that would materially affect downstream performance.

\paragraph{TOST non-inferiority confirms paradigm split.}
Pooling contrastive models, the mean gap is $-0.83\%$ (non-inferiority established: $p_{\text{TOST}} = 0.0009$ at $\delta = 2\%$). For non-contrastive models, the gap is $-3.41\%$ (non-inferiority fails: $p_{\text{TOST}} = 0.96$). The split holds across margins $\delta \geq 2$pp (Appendix~\ref{sec:tost_detail}). This formal statistical test confirms that random selection is not merely ``close to'' optimized selection for contrastive models---it is provably non-inferior within a 2pp margin.

\paragraph{Sequential $\approx$ Random ($|d| < 0.02$).}
The intrinsic dimension ordering carries no importance signal---off-the-shelf models do not exhibit Matryoshka-like structure. This contrasts with models explicitly trained with Matryoshka Representation Learning \citep{matryoshka2023}, where the first $k$ dimensions are specifically optimized to be informative. The absence of such structure in standard models confirms that truncation-ordering is a product of the training objective, not an inherent property of embedding spaces.

\paragraph{Anti-optimized confirms information exists.}
Despite the interchangeability of ``good'' methods, anti-optimized degrades to 84--91\% (Figure~\ref{fig:all_methods}). Information is distributed but not absent---it is spread so evenly that subset choice barely matters at moderate compression. The existence of this large anti-optimized gap (10--16pp below random) also establishes an important boundary condition: were the information truly concentrated in a small subset, anti-optimized selection would not degrade this much.

\section{Finding 2: Magnitude-Based Selection Fails}
\label{sec:finding2}

Magnitude rankings are uncorrelated with task importance ($\rho \approx 0$; Figure~\ref{fig:magnitude}(a)). For GTE-Large, magnitude is \emph{significantly worse} than random (96.74\% vs.\ 97.49\%, $p = 0.002$, $d = -0.27$). The top-128 chunks by magnitude share only 18.8\% with top-128 by task importance (Jaccard $= 0.10$): magnitude measures encoding activation, not downstream utility. This result is robust across model families---for every model tested, magnitude fails to outperform random selection, and for non-contrastive models (Roberta-Large, BART-Base) the degradation is even more severe. The failure of magnitude-based selection is particularly notable because it is the default pruning heuristic in the neural network compression literature \citep{han2015pruning}, suggesting that transfer of pruning intuition from weights to embeddings requires care.

\label{sec:finding3}

At dim=256, using any task's importance ranking to prune for any other task retains 94.8--100.4\% across 11 models (Table~\ref{tab:cross_task}), despite near-zero rank correlation ($\rho \approx 0.001$). This paradox is resolved by near-uniform importance: when all dimensions contribute roughly equally, any sufficiently large subset works (Figure~\ref{fig:transfer_paradox}). The paradox has a practical implication: practitioners do not need task-specific importance rankings for each downstream application. A single ranking computed on any reasonable proxy task suffices.

Transfer degrades sharply at aggressive compression: at $k=16$, retention drops to 44--50\% for contrastive and 33--35\% for non-contrastive models (Appendix~\ref{sec:transfer_detail}). This degradation pattern mirrors the main pruning results: at very aggressive compression, the ``any large subset works'' property breaks down because the retained subset is no longer large enough to capture the broadly distributed information.

\section{Finding 4: Stronger Baselines Also Fail}
\label{sec:finding4}

We test gradient-based importance, activation variance, and learned masks on 5 models (Table~\ref{tab:strong_baselines}; Appendix~\ref{sec:gradient_saliency}). For contrastive models, gains are marginal (+0.0 to +0.8\%). For non-contrastive models, gradient and activation-variance methods are \emph{actively harmful}: Roberta-Large drops from 102.9\% (random) to 93.4\% (gradient). Only the oracle reliably beats random, but it requires full task evaluation.

The failure of gradient-based methods for non-contrastive models is particularly striking. Gradient saliency identifies dimensions that are important for \emph{loss minimization} during training, but these dimensions do not align with importance for downstream task performance in the compressed embedding space. Similarly, activation variance captures which dimensions are most variable during inference, which does not translate to downstream utility. The learned mask approach, which directly optimizes a binary mask on the embedding dimensions, performs better for non-contrastive models (Roberta-Large: 102.0\%, InBedder: 98.5\%) but still does not consistently beat random for contrastive models.

\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

\paragraph{Near-uniform importance.}
We compute normalized Shannon entropy of chunk importance: $H_{\text{norm}} = -\frac{1}{\log_2 N}\sum_i p_i \log_2 p_i$ where $p_i = S_i / \sum_j S_j$. Values are near-maximum (contrastive: 0.993--0.994; non-contrastive: 0.989), confirming broadly distributed importance (Figure~\ref{fig:mechanism}). However, Shapley-based entropy (0.60--0.66) is much lower than standalone entropy (0.997--0.999), revealing that interaction-aware importance detects substantially more structure (Appendix~\ref{sec:importance_detail}).

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth,height=0.22\textheight,keepaspectratio]{fig7_redundancy_mechanism.png}
    \caption{(a) Normalized entropy near 1.0 for all models. (b) Cumulative importance rises gradually: top 50\% of chunks capture only 58--75\%.}
    \label{fig:mechanism}
\end{figure*}

\paragraph{Training paradigm predicts the gap.}
Linear regression (gap $\sim$ contrastive + params, $n=173$) shows the contrastive indicator is significant ($\beta = -2.77$, $p < 0.001$) while model size is not ($p = 0.37$). The paradigm gap is largest for STS tasks (1.26\% vs.\ 6.05\%) and smallest for classification (1.76\% vs.\ 4.05\%). Table~\ref{tab:inbedder_mechanism} shows non-contrastive models have lower entropy and higher Gini coefficients, providing a quantitative explanation: more concentrated importance distributions create more room for task-aware selection to exploit.

\paragraph{Compression ratio matters.}
The oracle--random gap grows with compression severity. At dim=256 (75\% reduction), the gap is +2--5\% for contrastive models. At dim=64 (94\% reduction), it widens to +6--12\%. At dim=16, random retention drops to 44--63\%, making any form of coordinate selection impractical. This scaling pattern suggests that random selection is adequate for moderate compression but task-aware methods become increasingly valuable as the budget shrinks. The transition point differs by model family: contrastive models tolerate more aggressive random pruning before the gap becomes practically significant.

\paragraph{Robustness.}
Random selection has manageable tail risk at dim $\geq$ 128 (P5 is 1--3\% below mean for contrastive models; Appendix~\ref{sec:random_variance}). Under domain shift (Table~\ref{tab:ood}), random is more robust than task-aware selection for contrastive models, avoiding negative transfer---OOD-optimized selection produces gaps of $-0.8$ to $+1.8$pp, comparable to random. For non-contrastive models, domain transfer can help or hurt depending on task similarity.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

For \emph{contrastive models}, random coordinate selection retains 95--98\% at 75\% compression, making it a strong zero-cost baseline. For \emph{non-contrastive models}, task-aware selection provides meaningful gains (+8--10\%). At dim=256, coordinate selection matches PCA and random projection for contrastive models, while preserving compatibility with existing retrieval infrastructure (Appendix~\ref{sec:retrieval_cost}). Magnitude-based pruning should be avoided regardless of model type.

\paragraph{Why does random work?}
The near-uniform entropy ($H_{\text{norm}} = 0.989$--$0.994$) means that each dimension (or chunk) contributes a roughly equal share of task performance. This is a direct consequence of contrastive training, which distributes semantic information across the full embedding space to maximize representational capacity. Non-contrastive models, trained with reconstruction or classification objectives, concentrate information more unevenly, creating exploitable structure for task-aware pruning.

\paragraph{When is random not enough?}
Three regimes demand task-aware selection: (1) non-contrastive models, where the oracle--random gap is +8--10\%; (2) aggressive compression (dim $\leq$ 64), where random retention drops below 93\% and variance increases sharply; and (3) domain shift scenarios where the target domain is unknown at compression time, though in this case even oracle selection struggles.

\paragraph{Relation to Matryoshka representations.}
Our sequential baseline tests whether off-the-shelf models exhibit Matryoshka-like structure. The answer is definitively no ($|d| < 0.02$ for sequential vs.\ random), confirming that the structured information distribution in Matryoshka-trained models \citep{matryoshka2023} is a product of the training objective, not an emergent property of embedding spaces.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
(1) Our oracle is an upper bound \emph{within standalone chunk-scoring methods}, not universally. Greedy forward selection slightly outperforms contiguous selection (Appendix~\ref{sec:non_contiguous}), but the gap is modest. (2) Conclusions are strongest at moderate compression; transfer degrades at dim $\leq$ 64. (3) The training-paradigm association is observational, not causal---we cannot rule out confounds from model size, data, or other architectural differences. (4) We evaluate on MTEB tasks only; results may differ for other embedding use cases (e.g., few-shot prompting, RAG).

\section{Conclusion}

Random dimension selection is a strong baseline for post-hoc compression of contrastive embedding models (+2.2--5.0\% oracle gap at dim=256), but the gap is substantially larger for non-contrastive models (+8.2--10.1\%). The near-uniform importance we observe (entropy $= 0.989$--$0.994$) explains why random works, but Shapley-based analysis (entropy $= 0.60$--$0.66$) shows this is partly a consequence of standalone scoring. These findings establish a practical rule: for contrastive models at moderate compression, random coordinate selection is the recommended default; for non-contrastive models or aggressive compression, task-aware selection provides meaningful gains. Future work should investigate whether these findings extend to multimodal embeddings and larger-scale retrieval benchmarks, and explore training-time interventions that preserve compression-friendly structure. Code and results will be released upon publication.

Greedy forward selection slightly outperforms the contiguous oracle on some tasks (Table~\ref{tab:non_contiguous_full}), but the differences are modest for contrastive models. The Jaccard overlap between oracle and greedy selections is very low (0.07--0.19), meaning the two methods select fundamentally different dimension subsets---yet achieve similar performance. This confirms that the \emph{specific} dimensions chosen matter less than the \emph{number} of dimensions retained, validating our use of contiguous selection in the main analysis.
